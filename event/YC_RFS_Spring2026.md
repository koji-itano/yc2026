## 1. Cursor for Product Managers — by Andrew Miklas

AI coding tools like Cursor and Claude Code excel at helping teams build software once requirements are clear, but coding is only one component. The critical challenge is figuring out **what to build** -- understanding user needs, evaluating markets, consolidating feedback, and deciding which problems merit attention.

Traditionally, product management outputs included requirements docs, design mockups, and task tickets for human engineers. Current teams use AI sporadically but no comprehensive system supports the entire discovery process.

"Imagine a tool where you upload customer interviews and product usage data, ask 'what should we build next?', and get the outline of a new feature complete with an explanation based on customer feedback as to why this is a change worth making."

The system would propose concrete UI modifications, data model adjustments, workflow improvements, and development tasks suitable for coding agents.

## 2. AI-Native Hedge Funds — by Charlie Holtz

"In the 1980s, a small group of funds started using computers to analyze markets. At the time it seemed silly, but quantitative trading is now obvious." Hedge funds are at a comparable inflection point with AI.

Successful funds won't simply integrate AI into existing strategies. Tomorrow's leading hedge funds will leverage AI to develop entirely new approaches where "alpha" originates -- "swarms of agents" analyzing 10-Ks, SEC filings, synthesizing research ideas, and executing trades. A fundamental reimagining, not an incremental enhancement.

## 3. AI-Native Agencies — by Aaron Epstein

Historically, agencies faced scaling challenges due to "low margins, slow manual work, and the only way to grow is to add more people." AI fundamentally changes the economics.

Rather than selling software to clients for self-service, future agencies can charge significantly higher fees by using AI internally to deliver finished products -- design firms creating custom work pre-contract, ad agencies producing video content without physical shoots, law firms completing documents in minutes rather than weeks.

"That's why agencies of the future will look more like software companies, with software margins."

## 4. Stablecoin Financial Services — by Daivik Goel

Stablecoins are rapidly becoming critical infrastructure for global finance, yet much of the financial services layer remains unbuilt. The GENIUS and CLARITY Acts are placing stablecoins in a unique position between DeFi and TradFi -- compliant but crypto-native.

This creates room for financial services offering DeFi benefits like better yield or access to tokenized real-world assets while operating under traditional compliance frameworks. "The regulatory window is open. The rails are being laid. It's the perfect time to build something that blurs the line between the two worlds."

## 5. AI for Government — by Tom Blomfield

"The first wave of AI companies has helped businesses and normal people fill in forms and complete online applications" with remarkable speed and accuracy. However, government agencies still manually process these submissions.

Government desperately needs AI tools to handle the surge coming. Digital government models exist in places like Estonia, though broader adoption is needed globally. Building this is a challenging sales landscape, yet initial customers typically become long-term partners with expandable contracts.

## 6. Modern Metal Mills — by Zane Hengsperger

American metal mills face systemic inefficiencies rooted in decades-old infrastructure. "Lead times of 8 to 30 weeks are normal" for rolled aluminum and steel tube purchases. Mills prioritize tonnage and utilization rather than speed, flexibility, or profitability.

The opportunity: modernizing mills through software-driven planning, real-time manufacturing execution systems, and contemporary automation -- simultaneously reducing lead times and enhancing margins, establishing domestically competitive, flexible metal production.

## 7. Large Spatial Models — by Ryan McLinko

While language models have dominated recent AI advancement, "their impact has been constrained to domains that can be expressed primarily through language." Current systems struggle with robust spatial reasoning, mental rotation, and physical manipulation understanding.

Building large-scale spatial models treating geometry as foundational primitives could enable AI to reason about and design real-world environments and objects. Success here would establish a new foundational AI model comparable to OpenAI or Anthropic's achievements.

## 8. Infra for Government Fraud Hunters — by Garry Tan

Government loses substantial funds annually to fraud. The False Claims Act's qui tam provision enables private citizens to file fraud lawsuits on behalf of government, sharing recovered amounts as incentive.

Current processes remain manual and protracted: "An insider tips off a law firm, and then the firm spends months or years manually pulling documents." Software should intelligently organize evidence, parse documents, trace corporate structures, and prepare complaint-ready materials, accelerating whistleblower firm effectiveness.

---

## 9. Make LLMs Easy to Train — by Gabriel Birnbaum

Language model training remains unnecessarily complicated despite substantial industry attention. Practitioners encounter broken SDKs, unreliable GPU infrastructure, open-source tooling bugs, and massive data management challenges.

Needed: APIs abstracting training complexity, databases managing terabyte-scale datasets, and development environments designed specifically for ML research. As post-training and specialization gain importance, these foundational tools could become essential infrastructure.

## 10. AI Guidance for Physical Work — by David Lieb

**This is the RFS most relevant to our project (Guidance OS).**

> "You know that scene in The Matrix, where Neo plugs a cable into the back of his head and wakes up a while later and says 'I know Kung Fu'? Physical work is about to get something similar -- not through brain implants, but through real-time AI guidance.
>
> Much of the AI conversation focuses on which desk jobs will get replaced. But for physical work -- stuff like field services, manufacturing, healthcare -- AI can't yet act in the world. What it can do is see, reason, and guide the human who does.
>
> Imagine wearing a small camera while an AI sees what you see and talks you through the job: 'turn off that valve', 'use the 3/8 inch wrench', 'that part looks worn, replace it'. Instead of needing months or years of training, workers can become effective immediately, with AI coaching them and accessing new skills when needed.
>
> **Why now?** Three things have converged. First, multimodal models can now see and reason about real-world situations reliably. Second, the hardware is already everywhere -- phones, AirPods, Smart Glasses. And third, skilled labor shortages make this economically urgent and a high wage job for millions of people.
>
> There are a few approaches you could take. The most obvious is to build this system and sell it to companies with existing workforces. Or, you could pick a vertical, like HVAC repair or nursing, and build a full-stack superpowered workforce. Or, you could build a platform that lets anyone sign up and become a skilled worker or start their own business.
>
> If you're interested in giving physical workers the same type of AI superpowers that Claude Code gives you, we'd love to see you apply."

### Key Takeaways for Our Project
- **Author**: David Lieb (YC Partner)
- **Exact verticals mentioned**: field services, manufacturing, healthcare, HVAC repair, nursing
- **Hardware**: phones, AirPods, Smart Glasses
- **3 approaches suggested**:
  1. Sell to companies with existing workforces (= our B2B SaaS model)
  2. Pick a vertical and build full-stack workforce
  3. Platform for anyone to become a skilled worker
- **Closing line**: "giving physical workers the same type of AI superpowers that Claude Code gives you"
